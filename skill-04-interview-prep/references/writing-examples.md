# Writing Examples: Interview Ready Reference
## Structural models for resume, cover letter, and interview prep documents

This file is the canonical style reference for Skills 02 and 04. When writing any document, consult this file to ensure structure, tone, and formatting match the approved examples.

---

## Resume Writing Style

### Professional Summary: approved pattern

The summary leads with functional identity and years of experience. It names the type of work and what it produces. It does not use adjectives like "passionate," "driven," or "results-oriented."

**Approved example (adapted from source):**
> Enterprise AI practitioner with a track record of turning broken workflows and manual processes into production AI solutions at scale. Designed and delivered department-level workshops enabling thousands of employees across Finance, Legal, HR, Sales, Procurement, Marketing, and Operations. Proficient in [platforms]. Governance and guardrail practitioner. US veteran.

**What this does:**
- Opens with functional identity, not a job title
- States track record (not claimed: implied through specificity)
- Closes with a differentiator (governance posture, veteran status)
- Zero filler words

---

### Core Competencies: approved pattern

Two rows, three columns. Group by function, not skill type. Each entry is: Category: Item • Item • Item.

**Approved structure:**
```
AI Platforms: Claude (Artifacts, Projects, Custom Skills) • Gemini Gems • OpenAI
Enablement: Enterprise Workshop Facilitation • Change Management • Adoption Metrics
Technical: Google Apps Script • M365 Connectors • Prompt Engineering
```

---

### Signature Projects / Key Achievements: approved pattern

Each project gets a name, one-line description, and two to three bullets. Numbers are required. If numbers do not exist, this section is omitted.

**Approved example (adapted):**

**Client Dossier Automation**
Replaced a multi-person manual research process with a validated AI artifact delivering sourced output in under 15 seconds.
- Eliminated a 3-to-5-day research lag from 60 to 120 sales calls per year
- Human review confirmed accuracy at or near 100% across all test runs
- Recovered direct research labor cost of approximately $14,400 per year

**What this does:**
- Name is a noun phrase describing the deliverable, not a project code
- Description is one sentence: what it replaced and what it delivers
- Bullets are outcomes, not activities
- Numbers are specific and attributed to a real source (time, cost, volume)

---

### Experience Bullets: approved pattern

**Format:** [Action verb] + [what you did] + [outcome or scale]

**Approved examples (adapted):**
- Designed and delivered 85 AI workshops enabling 1,500+ employees across 12+ departments
- Built enterprise AI guardrail frameworks using Google 4 Pillars and Kernel Method principles, covering PII protection, acceptable use, and security best practices
- Managed full platform lifecycle, SLA negotiation, and contract governance across four global communication platforms

**What these do:**
- Lead with a strong past-tense verb (Designed, Built, Managed: not Responsible for)
- State the scope or scale in the same clause as the action
- No em dashes. No semicolons. Commas and periods only.
- No bullets starting with "I"

**Common errors to avoid:**
- "Responsible for managing..." → "Managed..."
- "Helped to develop..." → "Developed..."
- "Worked with the team to..." → "Led cross-functional team to..."
- "Was instrumental in..." → State what you actually did

## Cover Letter Writing Style

### Opening paragraph: approved pattern

Does not begin with "I am writing to apply." Leads with the role name and immediate relevance. Establishes credibility in the first sentence.

**Approved example (adapted):**
> I am writing to apply for the Director, Enterprise AI & Enablement role at [Company]. For the past 18 months at [Employer] I have been doing exactly this kind of work: building enterprise AI strategy, establishing governance frameworks in partnership with InfoSec and senior leadership, enabling workforces at scale, and measuring what changes. When I read this job description, I recognized both the problems and the work.

**Note:** This opening uses "I am writing to apply": this is acceptable only when the following sentence immediately establishes specific relevance. The preferred alternative leads with the relevance directly:
> The Director, Enterprise AI & Enablement role at [Company] describes work I have been doing for the past 18 months at [Employer]: building enterprise AI strategy, establishing governance frameworks, enabling workforces at scale, and measuring what changes.

---

### Body paragraphs: approved pattern

Each body paragraph covers one experience cluster with a specific accomplishment. Numbers are included where they exist. The connection to the role requirement is explicit, not implied.

**Approved example (adapted):**
> No initiative in my program is completed without strong partnership with Engineering, CISO/InfoSec, and senior leadership. That cross-functional accountability is structural, not incidental. I also serve as an internal consultant to the CISO and Information Security team on AI acceptable use policy design and on the architecture of guardrail frameworks covering PII protection, intellectual property containment, and professional speech and output standards.

**What this does:**
- States the structural reality, not just the claim
- Anticipates a requirement (cross-functional partnership) and addresses it specifically
- No em dashes. Uses commas and periods to connect ideas.

---

### Closing paragraph: approved pattern

Restates fit without restating the resume. Expresses genuine motivation. Closes with confidence.

**Approved example (adapted):**
> The part of this job that is hardest to hire for is finding someone who has already closed the gap between AI experiments and AI capabilities in production, while maintaining the governance, security partnerships, and executive alignment that make those capabilities sustainable. I have been doing that work at [Employer]. I would welcome a conversation about how this experience could contribute to [Company].

**Note on em dash:** The original uses an em dash here. When rewriting, replace with a period or restructure: "...AI capabilities in production. Maintaining the governance..." or restructure to eliminate the break entirely.

## Interview Prep Document Style

### Core narrative block: approved pattern

Written to the candidate in second person. Delivered as a callout box in the Word document.

**Approved example (adapted):**
> Lead with this frame if the interviewer opens with "Tell me about yourself" or "Walk me through your background."
>
> SAY: "I've spent the last [X] years solving [the problem this role addresses] from the inside. Not deploying tools: building the governance, change management, and enablement infrastructure that makes adoption actually stick. [Role] is the same problem at the [customer/product/strategy] layer. I want to bring the perspective of someone who has lived it as a [buyer/builder/operator] to a team that's defining what good looks like in this space."

**What this does:**
- Specific to the role: not generic
- Anchored to the candidate's actual background
- Positions the candidate as a practitioner, not a job seeker
- Ends with forward motion, not backward-looking resume recap

---

### ANCHOR block: approved pattern

Each anticipated question gets an ANCHOR: not a script. The ANCHOR captures the substance. The candidate adapts the language.

**Approved format:**

> **[Question]**
>
> What they're testing: [one sentence explaining the underlying concern]
>
> ANCHOR: [3-5 sentences. What the candidate led with, what they did, what the result was. Specific to their background, not generic advice.]

**Approved example (adapted):**
> **Tell me about a time you drove AI adoption in a resistant organization.**
>
> What they're testing: Your ability to navigate change management at the human level, not just deploy technology.
>
> ANCHOR: At [Employer], I built the AI governance and enablement framework from scratch: starting with no formal program, no dedicated budget, and significant internal skepticism. The first challenge wasn't tools. It was trust. I ran use case discovery sessions with individual departments to surface what was actually slowing people down, then matched tools to those specific pain points rather than deploying capabilities and hoping for adoption. That sequencing: problem first, tool second: is what made it work.

---

### Questions for the interviewer: approved pattern

Each question gets a label for what the candidate is actually assessing by asking it. This turns the Q&A section into an intelligence-gathering tool, not just a courtesy.

**Approved format:**

| Question | What You're Assessing |
|---|---|
| [Question text] | [One sentence: what the answer reveals] |

**Approved example (adapted):**

| Question | What You're Assessing |
|---|---|
| How do you define what a strong hire looks like at 6 months vs. 12 months? | Whether they have a clear vision for the function. Also tells you what you'd actually be measured on. |
| What does the handoff between [upstream role] and [this role] look like in practice? | Role clarity. Ambiguity here means scope risk. |
| How does field feedback from this team get into the product roadmap? | Whether this is a voice role or an execution-only role. Critical for long-term fit. |

---

### Things to avoid: approved pattern

Written as direct statements, not hedged suggestions. One sentence each.

**Approved example (adapted):**
- Don't over-explain your resume. They've read it. Reference it briefly, then move to implication.
- Don't use "passionate about" or similar filler. Show it through specificity, not adjectives.
- Don't position yourself as still learning the space. You are operational in this space. Lead from that.
- Don't ask about compensation or benefits in this conversation. Wrong stage, wrong room.
