# Interview Prep Reference Example
## Skill 04: Interview Prep | Structural and Style Model

This is the canonical example of a correctly written hiring manager prep document produced by this skill. Use it as the structural and stylistic model for every prep document you generate. All names, companies, and roles are fictional. The format, tone, section logic, and writing discipline are the standard.

---

## What This Example Demonstrates

- Research-grounded interviewer profile with signal-to-action translation
- Explicit register shift table: recruiter screen vs. hiring manager conversation
- Core narrative written to the candidate in second person, delivered as a callout block
- ANCHOR blocks that capture substance without scripting word-for-word answers
- Questions for the interviewer with assessment subtext: not courtesy questions
- Things to avoid written as direct statements, not hedged suggestions
- Closing statement that restates fit without restating the resume
- No generic advice anywhere in the document: every line is specific to this candidate and this role

---

## Full Hiring Manager Prep Document Example

---

**MERIDIAN ANALYTICS**

Hiring Manager Prep Guide

VP of Customer Success: Interview with Jane Doe

John Doe  |  [Interview Date]

This conversation is not a skills screen. Jane Doe already knows you can do the job. This is about strategic alignment, how you think, and whether you would operate well inside her organization. Show up as a peer, not a candidate.

---

### 1. Who Is Jane Doe

Jane Doe is the Chief Customer Officer at Meridian Analytics, based in Austin, TX. She is your direct manager if you get the role. Duke MBA. She built the Customer Success function at Meridian from eight people to sixty over four years and owns the full post-sale revenue motion including expansion, retention, and professional services.

**WHAT HER PUBLIC PROFILE TELLS YOU**

| Signal | What It Means for You |
|---|---|
| Spoke at SaaStr on "CS as a Revenue Function" and publicly reframed churn as a product signal, not a CS failure | She thinks structurally about accountability. Lead your examples toward how you designed systems and held the right people accountable: not just how you personally saved accounts. |
| Posts consistently on LinkedIn about CS team development and career laddering for ICs | She invests in her people. She will want to know you can develop talent, not just manage outcomes. |
| Reposts content on AI in customer success workflows and tooling: practically focused, not hype | She is operationally engaged. She will want to know you have used these tools, not just read about them. |
| Inherited a CS org with low NPS and turned it around in 18 months | She has done the hard rebuild. She will respect candidates who have also operated in imperfect conditions. |

---

### 2. What This Conversation Is Actually About

Hiring manager conversations at this stage test three things. Know which one is happening at any given moment and respond accordingly.

| 1 | STRATEGIC FIT: Do you think about customer success and revenue retention the way she does? This is the dominant question. She will probe how you frame the problem, not just whether you have solved it. |
|---|---|
| 2 | TEAM FIT: Can you build and develop a CS team while also holding commercial accountability? Meridian's CS org owns net revenue retention. She needs leaders who can hold both. |
| 3 | EXECUTIVE REGISTER: Can you operate at the C-suite level with enterprise customers and internally with the product and sales organizations? She needs to see that in how you talk, not just in your resume. |

---

### 3. The Register Shift from the Recruiter Screen

The recruiter screen validated competence. This conversation requires you to shift register entirely.

| Recruiter Screen | Hiring Manager Conversation |
|---|---|
| Prove you can do the job | Demonstrate how you think about the work |
| Background and credentials | Strategic perspective and point of view |
| What you have done | How you would approach what is next |
| Answering questions | Having a conversation with a peer |
| Fit to role requirements | Fit to how she runs her organization |

**SHIFT:** Stop selling your resume. Start demonstrating your thinking. The best version of this conversation ends with Jane Doe feeling like she just talked to someone who already understands the problem she is trying to solve.

---

### 4. Your Core Narrative for This Conversation

Lead with this frame if she opens with "Tell me about yourself" or "Walk me through your background."

**SAY:** "I have spent the last eight years building customer success organizations that own commercial outcomes, not just satisfaction scores. The distinction matters because it changes how you hire, how you structure the team, and how you hold yourself accountable to the business. I have done that work in two different companies at very different stages: one turnaround, one build from scratch, and the through-line in both was the same: clarity on what the team is actually responsible for producing, and the systems to back it up. That is the work I want to bring to Meridian."

---

### 5. Anticipated Questions and How to Answer Them

**Tell me about a time you turned around a struggling customer success organization.**

What she is testing: Whether you can diagnose organizational problems accurately and make structural changes, not just motivational ones.

ANCHOR: At Calloway, I inherited a CS team with 67% gross retention and no defined ownership of expansion revenue. The first thing I did was diagnose whether the problem was talent, structure, or process: because the fix is completely different depending on the answer. It was structure. We had generalist CSMs owning 80-account books with no segmentation by revenue tier or growth potential. I redesigned the coverage model over 90 days, created a dedicated expansion motion with two senior CSMs, and tied compensation to net revenue retention for the first time. Gross retention was at 84% within 12 months. Net revenue retention crossed 110% the following year.

---

**How do you think about the relationship between customer success and product?**

What she is testing: Whether you treat product feedback as intelligence to be synthesized and acted on, or as a complaint log.

ANCHOR: CS should be the most structured voice of the customer into the product organization: not the loudest, the most structured. At Hartwell, I built a formal feedback loop with the product team: quarterly theme analysis across all CS accounts, prioritized by revenue impact, delivered in the same format every time so product could actually use it. Within six months we had two roadmap items that came directly from CS-sourced themes, and the product team started requesting the analysis rather than tolerating it. That shift happened because we made it easy for them to act on what we were telling them.

---

**How do you develop CS talent at the IC level?**

What she is testing: Whether you invest in people development or just manage to outcomes.

ANCHOR: I run structured career development conversations quarterly with every IC, separate from performance reviews. The conversation has three questions: where do you want to be in two years, what is standing between you and that, and what can I do to remove that. The answers tell me where to invest coaching time and where to adjust scope or exposure. At Meridian I would want to build a formal skills matrix for the CS IC track so development conversations are grounded in something concrete, not just manager intuition.

---

**How do you handle a major customer at risk of churning?**

What she is testing: Whether you have a systematic approach or whether you improvise under pressure.

ANCHOR: The first thing I do is separate the presenting problem from the root cause: they are almost never the same thing. A customer saying they are not seeing value is usually telling you something about onboarding, adoption, or executive alignment, not about the product. I use a structured health assessment: executive sponsor engagement, product adoption depth, support ticket velocity, and expansion history. Each one tells a different part of the story. Once I know the actual problem, I build a recovery plan with defined milestones and a named internal owner. I do not put the whole recovery on the CSM: that is how recoveries fail.

---

**Why Meridian, and why now?**

What she is testing: Genuine motivation versus job searching. She wants conviction.

ANCHOR: Meridian is at the stage where CS can either become a real competitive differentiator or stay a cost center with a better name. I have seen both outcomes and I know what makes the difference: it is whether CS has clear commercial accountability and the organizational structure to back it up. The work Jane Doe has already done to reframe CS as a revenue function tells me Meridian is on the right path. I want to bring experience from having done this in two different environments to help push that further and build something that compounds over time.

---

### 6. Your Questions for Jane Doe

Ask three to four. These carry as much signal as your answers. Choose based on where the conversation has gone.

| Question | What You Are Assessing |
|---|---|
| How do you define what a great VP of CS looks like at 6 months versus 18 months? | Whether she has a clear picture of the role or is still forming it. Also tells you what you would actually be measured on. |
| How does CS field intelligence get into the product roadmap: is there a formal channel or is it relationship-dependent? | Whether the feedback loop is structural or personality-driven. Personality-driven loops break when people leave. |
| Where does the boundary between CS and Sales sit on expansion and renewal conversations? | Role clarity. Ambiguity here creates internal conflict and customer confusion. You need a clean answer. |
| What is the hardest part of the customer conversation you are seeing right now: the one that does not have a clean playbook yet? | Positions you as a peer. Also gives you intelligence on what she is solving for that you can speak to directly. |

---

### 7. Things to Avoid

- Do not over-explain your resume. She has read it. Reference it briefly and move to implication.
- Do not use "passionate about customer success" or similar filler. Show it through specificity.
- Do not position yourself as someone who is learning to hold commercial accountability. You already do. Lead from that.
- Do not ask about compensation or benefits in this conversation. Wrong stage, wrong room.
- Do not let the conversation become one-directional. This is a dialogue: you should be asking as much as answering.
- Do not undersell the scale of your prior work by being modest. State the numbers and let them stand.

---

### 8. How to Close the Conversation

If given the chance to close, or when she asks if you have any final thoughts:

**CLOSE:** "I have spent eight years building CS organizations that own real commercial outcomes, and I have done it in conditions that were not clean: turnarounds, rapid scaling, and cross-functional alignment that had to be earned rather than given. What draws me to this role specifically is that Meridian is at the point where the CS function can become genuinely differentiated, and I think the foundation Jane Doe has built makes that possible in a way it would not be at most companies. I would want to be part of building what comes next."

Peer-level. Specific. Forward-looking. You have done the work: let the conversation reflect that.

---

*John Doe  |  Meridian Analytics VP CS: Hiring Manager Prep*

## Annotation Notes for Skill 04

**Section 1: Interviewer Profile:** Always research-grounded. The signal table translates public data (posts, talks, reposts, career history) into actionable guidance for the candidate. If insufficient public data exists on the interviewer, flag it explicitly and focus on role-level signals instead. Never fabricate signals.

**Section 2: What This Conversation Is Actually About:** Three modes, clearly labeled. The candidate needs to know which mode is active at any given moment and respond accordingly. This section makes that explicit rather than leaving it implicit.

**Section 3: Register Shift:** The table format is required. This is the most actionable section for candidates who have only done recruiter screens: they need to see the difference spelled out, not described.

**Section 4: Core Narrative:** Written to the candidate in second person. Delivered as a callout block in the Word document. Specific to this candidate's background and this role. Not a generic elevator pitch template.

**Section 5: ANCHOR Blocks:** Not scripts. The candidate adapts the language to the live conversation. The ANCHOR captures: what the candidate led with, what they did, and what the outcome was. Every ANCHOR is grounded in a specific example from the candidate's background: never generic advice. If the candidate has not provided a strong example for a question type, flag it and ask for one before writing the ANCHOR.

**Section 6: Questions for the Interviewer:** Each question has assessment subtext. The subtext tells the candidate what the answer will reveal about the role, the manager, and the organization. These are intelligence-gathering questions, not courtesy questions.

**Section 7: Things to Avoid:** Direct statements. One sentence each. No hedging. No "you might want to consider avoiding." Just state it.

**Section 8: Closing Statement:** Restates fit without restating the resume. Expresses genuine motivation. Closes with forward motion, not gratitude. The closing line in italics is a tone anchor for the document: peer-level, specific, earned.

**What is not in this document:**
- Generic interview tips that apply to any candidate for any role
- Scripts the candidate should memorize and recite
- Motivational language or encouragement
- Any section that could have been written without knowing who the candidate is or who the interviewer is
